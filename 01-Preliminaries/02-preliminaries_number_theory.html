The Division Theorem (a.k.a. Division Algorithm)

Back to integers‚Äîdivision‚Äôs messy, but predictable. Here‚Äôs the golden rule:

For any integer \(a\) and positive integer \(b > 0\), there are unique integers \(q\) (quotient) and \(r\) (remainder) such that:

[ a = b \cdot q + r \quad \text{where } 0 \leq r < b ]

  • Example: \(23 \div 5\):
    • \(23 = 5 \cdot 4 + 3\) ‚Üí \(q = 4\), \(r = 3\).
  • Negative Twist: \(-7 \div 3\):
    • \(-7 = 3 \cdot (-3) + 2\) ‚Üí \(q = -3\), \(r = 2\).

This is the launchpad for modular arithmetic‚Äîcrypto‚Äôs everything!

Factorization & Prime Numbers: The Hard Problem

A factor divides a number cleanly:

  • \(6\)‚Äôs factors: \(1, 2, 3, 6\).
  • Prime: Only divisible by 1 and itself‚Äî\(2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \dots\).

Why primes rule:

  • They‚Äôre multiplication‚Äôs ‚Äúatoms.‚Äù
  • Every number splits into primes uniquely (e.g., \(12 = 2^2 \cdot 3\)).
  • Factoring big numbers (like 2048-bit RSA keys)? Super hard!

Hermes Whispers: ‚ÄúFast to multiply, slow to un-multiply‚Äîthat‚Äôs the trapdoor magic!‚Äù

GCD: Greatest Common Divisor
  • \(\gcd(a, b)\): Biggest number dividing both \(a\) and \(b\).
  • \(\gcd(24, 36) = 12\).
  • \(\gcd(a, b) = 1\) ‚Üí \(a\) and \(b\) are coprime (no common factors but 1).
Euclidean Algorithm (for GCD)

Fast trick: [ \gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \mod b) ] Repeat until \(b = 0\).

  • Example: \(\gcd(48, 18)\):
    • \(48 \mod 18 = 12\)
    • \(18 \mod 12 = 6\)
    • \(12 \mod 6 = 0\) ‚Üí Done! \(\gcd = 6\).
LCM: Least Common Multiple
  • Smallest number both \(a\) and \(b\) divide into.
  • Formula: [ \text{lcm}(a, b) = \frac{|a \cdot b|}{\gcd(a, b)} ]
  • \(\text{lcm}(12, 18) = \frac{12 \cdot 18}{6} = 36\).

From Infinite to Finite: Why Cryptography Needs Boundaries

\(\mathbb{Z}\) and \(\mathbb{Q}\) go on forever‚Äîcool, but impractical:

Welcome to Modular Arithmetic

Using the Division Theorem, we carve \(\mathbb{Z}\) into finite sets:

  • \(\mathbb{Z}_n\): \({0, 1, 2, \dots, n-1}\).

  • Add, subtract, multiply‚Äîthen take \(\mod n\) to loop back.

  • In \(\mathbb{Z}_5\):

    • \(3 + 4 = 7 \equiv 2 \pmod{5}\).
    • \(2 \times 4 = 8 \equiv 3 \pmod{5}\).
    • \(-1 \equiv 4 \pmod{5}\).

Athena Notes: ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs clock math‚Äîhit the max, wrap around. This is the backbone of encryption and hashing!‚Äù
